A few years ago, I worked on a project that still comes back to me—not because it was wildly successful, but because of how much it demanded from me. The company was Arcrome Technologies. Their idea was SelfieStar: a kiosk application for movie theatres where people could interact with their favorite stars.

THE CONCEPT

The core idea was simple on paper: A large touch-screen kiosk. A "Let’s Start" button. A slick animation. You swipe to pick a movie, pay via card or cash, and then take pictures with celebrities from that movie. But simplicity on paper often hides complexity underneath.

THE COMPLEXITY

This wasn't just software. It was physics, optics, and user perception. We had to handle payments (card swiping + manual cash codes), guidance (telling users exactly where to stand), and lighting match-ups. The founders weren't tech-savvy, so they relied on me to define what was technically possible.

THE TECH STACK

I chose Unity as the foundation. It gave us real-time rendering, animation control, and hardware flexibility. To build the "touch" system, we paired a 55-inch TV with a 55-inch IR touch frame connected to a PC. We added a DSLR camera for high-quality capture. It was a massive, custom-built hardware beast.

THE EXECUTION

I wasn't just coding; I was troubleshooting hardware and designing flows. I built the UI with **DOTween** for cinematic transitions—standout elements like a massive "S" animation that filled the screen. For the backend, we used Strapi (Headless CMS) on AWS. The Caching System: Video files were gigabytes in size. I designed a system where kiosks would download content during boot-up or maintenance windows to avoid streaming issues.

THE MAGIC: REAL-TIME COMPOSITING

The heart of the experience. I wrote a custom shader to perform chroma keying in real-time. We took pre-recorded celebrity footage (green screen) and the live camera feed, layering them together to create the illusion that the user was standing right next to the star. Tweaking thresholds and lighting spills until it looked real.

THE LESSON

The prototype worked. We pitched to investors. But the goalposts kept moving ("What else can it do?"). The project eventually reached a quiet conclusion, but it taught me more than many successes did. It forced me to think end-to-end—hardware, software, users, and reality. It reminded me that building something real is messy, but valuable.
